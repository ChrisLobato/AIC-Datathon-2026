## Description
Our winning submission for the Education Track for a 24 hour datathon in which we did an analysis of United States Higher Education insitutions.


## Goal
Analyze the value of colleges by examining the relationship between cost, and post-graduation earnings using the U.S. department of education College Scorecard dataset. Now more than ever the value of higher education has been up for debate with increasing unemployment/underemployment rates and cost of attendance. We aim to see if there are any quantifiable differences between the institutions to see if what school you go to matters. Additionally we aim to see if there are factors that may directly contribute to financial outlook post college.

## Repo overview
The **The Hidden Cost of Higher Education.pdf** is our presentation submission which was presented infront of the Stony Brook University faculty judging the competition. In this presentation we included the visual insights of the project, like correlation matrix, summary statisitcs, and cluster graphs. **Datathon 2026.ipynb** is a python notebook of the code used to clean the data and build our models.

## Conclusions 
Overall, we found that the problem is a lot more nuanced than we initially believed. While it is easy to quantify institutions and students in terms of statistics like return on investment, many of the smaller details get lost in all of the data. We found that economic class could be a large determining factor for financial success post‑college, as many institutions who boasted higher average earnings were also institutions that did not award as much Pell Grant funding. Perhaps supporting this we observed an interesting positive correlation between the average SAT scores of admitted students and their median earnings.
Over the past few years, there has been a lot of discourse surrounding how SATs promote racial and economic biases, pushing many institutions to adopt test optional policies. So our findings reveal that there are additional factors that need to be investigated. In the future, we would like to examine statistics regarding economic background and racial distributions of students admitted into these schools to determine whether there is a larger systemic issue introducing bias.
